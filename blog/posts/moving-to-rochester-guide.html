Moving to Rochester — the complete newcomer's guide to living in the city

📅 1 June 2026 🕐 6 min read 👥 Medway & Kent Removals Team

Rochester has quietly become one of Kent's most attractive places to live, offering an irresistible blend of historic charm, modern amenities, and excellent transport links to London. Whether you're drawn by the stunning cathedral, the affordable housing compared to other parts of the South East, or simply the friendly community atmosphere, moving to Rochester Kent represents a smart choice for families, professionals, and retirees alike. After helping hundreds of families relocate to this beautiful Medway town, we've gathered everything you need to know about making Rochester your new home.

Why People Choose Rochester

Rochester's appeal lies in its unique character. You're getting a historic city with Norman cathedral spires and medieval streets, yet you're just 30 minutes from central London by high-speed rail. The High Street feels authentic rather than commercialised, local independent shops sit alongside well-known chains, and you can walk along the River Medway or explore Rochester Castle within minutes of leaving your front door.

Property prices remain reasonable compared to similar commuter towns. A three-bedroom family home that would cost ��600,000 in nearby areas often sells for £350,000-£450,000 in Rochester, making it particularly attractive for first-time buyers and growing families looking for more space.

The schools are excellent, both state and private options, and the sense of community is genuine. People stop to chat in the street, local events bring neighbours together, and there's a real pride in the area's heritage without it feeling stuffy or unwelcoming to newcomers.

Understanding Rochester's Neighborhoods

Rochester City Centre

Living in the heart of Rochester puts you within walking distance of everything. The High Street offers daily shopping, the cathedral provides a stunning backdrop, and you're minutes from Rochester station. Properties here range from converted Georgian townhouses to modern apartments, with many featuring period details like original fireplaces and high ceilings.

The area can get busy during tourist season, especially around Dickens Festival time, but most residents find this adds to rather than detracts from the atmosphere. Parking can be challenging, so consider this if you rely heavily on your car.

Borstal and Wouldham Areas

These residential areas offer excellent value for families seeking more space. Borstal, despite its historical associations, is actually a pleasant area with good schools and parks. Many properties here are 1930s builds with generous gardens, perfect for families with children.

Wouldham, technically a separate village but closely connected to Rochester, provides a more rural feel while maintaining easy access to the city. Properties tend to be larger, and you're close to excellent walking routes along the North Downs Way.

Strood Connection

While technically separate, Strood forms part of the wider Rochester area and offers more affordable housing options. The Temple Waterfront development has brought modern apartments and improved facilities, and the transport links are excellent with both Strood and Rochester stations nearby.

Transport and Commuting Options

Rochester's transport connections are genuinely excellent, which explains much of its popularity among people moving to Rochester Kent from London and other parts of the South East.

Rail Services

Rochester station sits on the high-speed line to London, with services reaching St Pancras International in around 35 minutes during peak times. Standard services to Victoria take about 45 minutes. For commuters, this makes Rochester one of the best-connected towns in Kent.

Services run frequently throughout the day, typically every 15-30 minutes depending on the time and destination. Season ticket costs are significant but still represent good value when you consider the journey time and housing price differences.

Road Links

The M2 motorway runs close to Rochester, providing quick access to the M25 and broader motorway network. Journey times to London by car vary dramatically depending on traffic, but you can typically reach the capital in 45-90 minutes.

The A2 offers an alternative route that passes through some lovely Kent countryside, though it's generally slower than the motorway option.

Local Transport

Local bus services connect Rochester with nearby Medway towns including Chatham, Gillingham, and Strood. Many residents find they can manage daily life without a car, especially if living near the city centre, though most families do choose to run a vehicle for convenience.

Schools and Education

Education options represent one of Rochester's strongest selling points for families. The area offers outstanding state schools alongside respected private institutions, giving parents genuine choice regardless of budget.

Primary Education

Warren Wood Primary School consistently receives excellent Ofsted ratings and is highly sought after among local families. St Margaret's Rochester C of E Primary offers faith-based education with strong academic results, while Hundred of Hoo Academy provides modern facilities and innovative teaching approaches.

Admission to popular primary schools can be competitive, so research catchment areas carefully when house hunting. Many families specifically choose their Rochester location based on school proximity.

Secondary Schools

The Rochester Grammar School for girls and Sir Joseph Williamson's Mathematical School for boys are both selective schools with outstanding reputations. Competition for places is fierce, but they offer exceptional academic opportunities.

For comprehensive education, The Hundred of Hoo Academy and Chatham Grammar School (mixed) provide excellent alternatives with strong results and good pastoral care.

Private Education

King's School Rochester, one of the world's oldest schools, offers exceptional private education with stunning cathedral setting. Rochester Independent College provides sixth-form education with small class sizes and intensive university preparation.

Shopping and Amenities

Daily life in Rochester is refreshingly straightforward. The High Street provides most essentials, from groceries at the local Co-op and independent grocers to banking, pharmacy services, and casual dining options.

For larger shopping trips, Blue Water shopping centre sits about 15 minutes away by car and offers every major retailer plus cinema and restaurants. Chatham, just a few minutes away, provides additional supermarket options including Tesco, ASDA, and Sainsbury's.

Rochester's independent shops add real character to daily shopping. The Saturday market brings local producers and crafters to the High Street, while antique shops and bookstores provide browsing opportunities you simply don't find in more commercialised areas.

Leisure and Recreation

Historic Attractions

Living in Rochester means having spectacular historic sites on your doorstep. Rochester Cathedral, built in 1077, provides a magnificent focal point, while Rochester Castle offers stunning views across the Medway valley.

The annual Dickens Festival transforms the city into Victorian England for a weekend, complete with costumed characters and period entertainment. Rather than feeling like a tourist trap, most residents embrace these events as genuine community celebrations.

Outdoor Activities

The River Medway provides excellent walking routes, while the nearby North Downs offer more challenging hikes with spectacular views. Capstone Country Park, just outside Rochester, features woodland walks, open parkland, and children's play areas.

For sports enthusiasts, local facilities include tennis courts, football pitches, and the Medway Park leisure centre in nearby Gillingham. The river supports rowing and sailing clubs for those interested in water sports.

Cultural Life

Rochester's cultural scene punches above its weight for a city of its size. The Corn Exchange hosts regular concerts and performances, while local pubs often feature live music. The annual Sweeps Festival brings Morris dancing and traditional music to the streets each May.

Proximity to London means you can easily access world-class museums, theatres, and concerts while enjoying the slower pace of life that Rochester provides day-to-day.

Healthcare and Services

Healthcare provision in Rochester is generally good, though like most areas, you'll need to register with local services promptly after moving. Eastgate House Medical Centre and Star Hill Family Practice both accept new patients regularly, though it's worth contacting them early in your moving process.

Medway Maritime Hospital in nearby Gillingham provides accident and emergency services plus specialist care. For routine healthcare, Rochester offers several dental practices, opticians, and pharmacies within easy walking distance of most residential areas.

Council services are managed by Medway Council, which handles everything from refuse collection to planning applications. Local residents generally report good service levels, and the council tax rates remain reasonable compared to many Kent authorities.

Cost of Living

Understanding the true cost of living in Rochester helps with realistic budgeting for your move. Housing represents the largest expense for most families, but costs remain significantly lower than London while offering much more space.

Typical monthly expenses include council tax (£100-£200 depending on property band), utilities (£80-£150 for gas and electricity), and water rates (£30-£50). Internet and phone services are readily available from all major providers.

Food shopping costs are typical for Kent, with local independent shops sometimes costing slightly more than supermarkets but offering better quality and personal service. Most families find their overall cost of living decreases significantly compared to London, even accounting for commuting costs.

Community and Social Life

Rochester's community spirit is one of the factors that keeps people here long-term. Local groups cater to most interests, from book clubs and walking groups to amateur dramatics and historical societies.

The Rochester and Strood Camera Club, Rochester Choral Society, and various sports clubs provide regular social opportunities. Many residents mention how easy it is to meet like-minded people, particularly compared to larger urban areas where communities can feel more fragmented.

Local churches, regardless of your faith background, often serve as community hubs hosting everything from coffee mornings to charitable events. The Cathedral in particular plays an important role in city life, hosting concerts and community gatherings throughout the year.

Practical Moving Considerations

When planning your move to Rochester, timing can make a significant difference to your experience. School term times affect both property availability and removal costs, so consider these factors in your planning.

Parking restrictions in the city centre require careful consideration. Some streets have resident permit schemes, while others rely on time-limited parking. Factor this into your property search if you rely heavily on your car.

Council services registration should happen quickly after moving. This includes council tax, electoral roll registration, and any benefit transfers. Medway Council's website provides clear guidance, but allow several weeks for everything to process.

Local utility connections are generally straightforward, though some period properties may require electrical or plumbing updates to meet modern standards. Factor these potential costs into your moving budget.
